### **YOUR STORY: 1895-1926**

#### **CHILDHOOD (1895-1913): The Reader**

**Born:** Boston, 1895, to schoolteacher parents (modest middle-class family).

**Father:** Thomas Fletcher (history teacher, died 1910 when Maria was 15).  
**Mother:** Adelaide Fletcher (English teacher, still alive in Boston).

**Defining Moment (Age 8):**
Witnessed factory fire (Triangle Shirtwaist-style tragedy). Saw newspaper reporter interviewing survivors, writing down their stories. Thought: *"Someone needs to tell their truth."*

**Education:**
- Exceptional student (top of class)
- Voracious reader (three books per week)
- Won essay competitions (local newspapers published them)
- Father encouraged her: *"Don't let anyone tell you girls can't write the news."*

**Father's Death (1910):**
Heart attack. Maria was 15. Found solace in writing. Kept journal obsessively. Decided: *"I'll tell the stories he can't anymore."*

**1913-1916: College** (Boston University, English & History)

**1916-1917: Junior Reporter** (*Boston Globe*)

**1922-1923: The Reggie Ashford Investigation**

**How It Started (October 1922):**

Anonymous tip: *"Look into Reginald Ashford III. Real estate deals. Something wrong."*

**Maria's Research (Oct 1922 - June 1923, 8 months):**

**What She Discovered:**
1. **Property Fraud:** Reggie bought tenement buildings, evicted tenants, sold for profit
2. **Bribery:** Paid city officials to rezone neighborhoods (making his properties valuable)
3. **Predatory Loans:** Gave loans to desperate families, foreclosed when they couldn't pay
4. **Blackmail:** Used private detectives to dig up dirt on business rivals
5. **Tax Evasion:** Offshore accounts, shell companies (illegal even in 1920s)

**Evidence:**
- Bank records (leaked by sympathetic bank employee)
- Testimony from 12 evicted families
- Documents showing bribes (photocopied from city hall - risky)
- Letter from Reggie to lawyer detailing blackmail scheme

**Maria's Notebook:**
200 pages of notes. Names, dates, amounts. Airtight case.

---

**June 15, 1923: "The Ashford Empire: Built on Greed and Lies"**

**The Article:**
- Published front page, *New York Herald*
- 5,000 words
- Named Reggie specifically
- Detailed his methods
- Called for federal investigation
- Quote: *"Men like Reginald Ashford III profit from the suffering of the poor. This must end."*

**Immediate Reaction:**
- National attention (reprinted in 15 other papers)
- Public outrage
- Calls for investigation
- Maria's editor: *"Best journalism I've seen in 20 years."*

**Maria felt:** Triumphant. This is what journalism is FOR.

---

**June 16-17, 1923: Reggie's Response**

**Step 1:** Hired expensive lawyers (five of them).

**Step 2:** Filed libel lawsuit (claimed article was FALSE, DAMAGED reputation).

**Step 3:** Used connections (judges, politicians, media owners).

**The Legal Battle (June-November 1923):**

**Maria's Defense:**
- Truth is absolute defense against libel
- She HAD evidence
- Herald provided legal team

**Reggie's Strategy:**
- Challenge evidence sourcing (bank employee was "unauthorized")
- Claim documents were "stolen" (technically true - photocopied without permission)
- Pressure Herald owners (threaten advertisers, business relationships)
- Find technicality (evicted families signed "settlements" with non-disclosure clauses)

---

**November 1923: The Verdict**

**Judge ruled:** Reggie WINS on technicality.

**Reasoning:**
- Some evidence obtained "improperly" (tainted)
- Families' testimony "inadmissible" (NDAs)
- One date wrong in article (minor error, but enough)

**Reality:** Reggie's wealth and connections bought the verdict.

**Outcome:**
- Maria ordered to pay $25,000 damages (more than 5 years salary)
- Article retracted
- Reggie "exonerated" (in legal sense only)

---

**November 1923 - January 1924: The Destruction**

**Immediate Consequences:**

**Financial:**
- Can't pay $25,000 (negotiated down to $10,000, still crushing)
- Mother mortgaged house to help (Maria felt HORRIBLE)
- In debt for years

**Professional:**
- Herald fired her (pressure from ownership - Reggie's business associates)
- Blacklisted (Reggie sent letters to every major publication: "Don't hire her")
- No newspaper would touch her
- Award rescinded (Press Club - Reggie's friends on board)

**Personal:**
- Reputation smeared ("sloppy journalist," "bitter woman," "reckless")
- Former colleagues avoided her (afraid of association)
- Felt betrayed (journalism failed her after she served it faithfully)

**Depression (Jan-March 1924):**
- Stayed in tiny apartment for weeks
- Thought about quitting (maybe teach? like mother?)
- But couldn't give up (too stubborn, too angry)
